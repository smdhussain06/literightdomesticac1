Module 1

Introduction to the World of Lighting Design

Duration: 30 minutes  |  Objective: Understand what lighting design is, why it matters, and the methodology you'll follow.

Lesson 1.1 — What Is Lighting Design? (10 mins)

Lighting design is the deliberate art and science of shaping how light falls within a space. It goes far beyond choosing light fixtures — it's about crafting an experience.

  • Lighting design sits at the intersection of architecture, interior design, physics, and psychology
  • A lighting designer's job is to support the message of the interior architecture
  • Professional lighting design involves three phases:
    1. Conceptualization — defining the idea, the rules, the mood
    2. Technique — choosing the right light sources and fixtures
    3. Documentation — communicating through drawings, plans, and presentations
💡 Core Principle: "We always start by conceptualizing our spaces as a black box, to which we subsequently add light."

Lesson 1.2 — The LumLum Methodology (10 mins)

StepWhat You DoDeliverable
1. ConceptualizeDefine your core idea, set of rules, and the sensation you want to provokeMood board, concept statement, rule set
2. TechniqueSelect luminaires, specify their properties, understand installationFixture schedule, technical specifications
3. DocumentDraw the light in plan, section, elevation; present to clientDrawings, conceptual presentation, plans

Lesson 1.3 — Case Study Preview: Madre Café (10 mins)

Throughout this course, we'll reference the Madre Café project — a café in a Mexican Porfiriato-era mansion.

  • Warm color temperature (3000K) throughout
  • Concept of asymmetry — balanced light from non-identical sources
  • Perimeter lighting to reveal wall texture
  • Dramatic light arch as the primary focal element
  • Three artificial layers + one natural layer (skylight)
  • LAMP Awards finalist project
📝 Assignment: Identify a space you'll use as your personal project. Take photos from multiple angles.

Module 2

Understanding Light & Perception

Duration: 45 minutes  |  Objective: Grasp the physics of light, human perception, and fundamental metrics.

Lesson 2.1 — What Is Light? (15 mins)

Light is electromagnetic radiation visible to the human eye (380–780 nm).

Four Behaviors of Light on Surfaces

BehaviorDescriptionExample
ReflectionLight bounces off a surfaceMirror, polished marble
AbsorptionSurface absorbs light energyDark matte walls
TransmissionLight passes throughClear glass
RefractionLight bends between materialsPrism, water surface
💡 Key Insight: The same light source can produce completely different effects depending on the material it hits. Light design is inseparable from material design.

Lesson 2.2 — The Observer (15 mins)

  • Rods — sensitive to brightness, active in low-light (scotopic vision)
  • Cones — sensitive to color, active in well-lit conditions (photopic vision)
  • Mesopic vision — the transition zone (dusk, dimmed interiors)

Three Types of Visual Adaptation

  1. Light adaptation — dark to bright (~5 seconds)
  2. Dark adaptation — bright to dark (up to 30 minutes)
  3. Chromatic adaptation — adjusting to different color temperatures

Module 4

Conceptualization — Designing with Light

Duration: 60 minutes  |  Objective: Master Richard Kelly's concepts, layers, hierarchies, and build your core concept.

Lesson 4.1 — Richard Kelly's Three Concepts (20 mins)

1. Ambient Luminescence

General, uniform background illumination. Non-directional, soft, low contrast. Makes a space feel safe and open.

2. Focal Glow

Directed, concentrated light on a specific area. Creates hierarchy and draws attention. Track lights, pendants, desk lamps.

3. Play of Brilliants

Sparkle, glitter, decorative points of light. Adds visual interest and luxury. Chandeliers, candles, neon signs.

Lesson 4.2 — Layers and Hierarchies (20 mins)

Madre Café — 4 Layers

LayerFunctionType
1Light arch (primary detail)Diffuse, warm, wall-washing
2Table lighting (task)Focal, downward, warm
3Perimeter wall lightingIndirect, texture-revealing
4Skylight (natural)Daylight, dynamic, zenith

Lesson 4.3 — Developing Your Core Concept (20 mins)

Answer two questions: "What do I want the lighting to achieve?" and "What sensation do I want to provoke?"

Example Design Rules (Café CDMX)

RuleRationale

[VISUAL: Gallery spaces, light installations, sun paths]

We also added one artificial light source to create contrast and observe the difference between static artificial light and the ever-moving natural light.

This was a self-funded project. Part of the budget came from my own pocket, part from a sponsoring mentor who saw the intention behind the work, and part from the gallery. The investment paid off in ways money can't measure.

When I arrived in Spain, I discovered my competition wasn't just individual designers — these were entire companies with 50, even 200 employees. We were the only Latin Americans among the finalists.

We didn't win the grand prize. But the experience taught me that the intention of the light is what truly matters, not the size of your company or budget.

Every designer is shaped by the people who inspire them. In this lesson, I want to share the artists, designers, and thinkers who have profoundly influenced my approach to lighting design. I've selected five key influences — some are lighting designers, some are artists, and some work at the intersection of light, color, and perception.

SCENE 2James Turrell1:00–3:30
[VISUAL: Turrell's installations — Roden Crater, sky spaces]

First, James Turrell. Turrell is an American artist who works directly with light and space. His work doesn't just use light as illumination — light IS the medium. He creates installations where you literally cannot tell where the light begins and the wall ends.

In his sky spaces, the sky becomes a canvas of changing light. You sit watching the sky shift from blue to purple to deep orange, and your entire understanding of light changes. Turrell teaches us that light is not just a tool for visibility — it's a material as powerful as concrete, glass, or steel.

SCENE 3Olafur Eliasson3:30–5:30
[VISUAL: Weather Project, Your Rainbow Panorama]

Next, Olafur Eliasson — a Danish-Icelandic artist who creates large-scale public installations. His most famous work, "The Weather Project" at the Tate Modern, created an artificial sun inside a massive turbine hall. Thousands of visitors lay on the floor gazing upward.

What I admire most is the public nature of his work. His art is accessible to everyone. He takes the conversation about light out of galleries and into civic spaces. This idea — that good lighting should serve everyone — deeply influences my approach.

SCENE 4Hella Jongerius5:30–8:30
[VISUAL: Color Catchers, Breathing Colors exhibition]

Hella Jongerius is a Dutch designer and director of color research at Vitra. Her project "Breathing Colors" includes "Color Catchers" — large faceted vessels that reveal different colors depending on the angle of light.

The color doesn't change. The surface doesn't change. What changes is the angle of the light. This demonstrates: color is not constant, light is not constant, observers are not constant. When we delve into color, we're inherently discussing light. Every surface is a partnership between material and light.

SCENE 5Kaoru Mende8:30–12:30
[VISUAL: LPA projects, Light Detectives, nighttime cities]

Kaoru Mende is a Japanese lighting designer and director of Lighting Planners Associates. He runs "Light Detectives" — a project where they tour cities at night, photograph, and analyze the quality of light. They explore how culture affects the image of a city after dark.

From Mende, I learned the concept of heroes and villains of light. A "hero" might be a subtle candle creating intimacy. A "villain" is harsh light that blinds you, or prevents you from feeling anonymous in a bar that should feel mysterious. This concept is one of the most practical tools I use when evaluating any space.

🎬 What Is Light?

Duration: ~11 min · 6 scenes

▶

Video 2.1 What Is Light?

~11 min

SCENE 1Opening0:00–0:30
[VISUAL: Electromagnetic spectrum graphic]

Welcome to Unit 2. Before we can design with light, we need to understand what it is, how it behaves, and how we measure it.

SCENE 2The Nature of Light0:30–2:30
[VISUAL: Wave animations, prism dispersing white light]

Light is electromagnetic radiation — the narrow band our eyes can detect, from 380 nanometers (violet) to 780 nanometers (red). Different light sources emit different spectral distributions. A warm incandescent emphasizes red and yellow wavelengths. A cool LED emphasizes blue. This spectral content affects how colors appear in our spaces.

SCENE 3Units of Measurement2:30–6:00
[VISUAL: Animated measurement diagrams]

Luminous intensity (candelas) — light in a particular direction. Luminous flux (lumens) — total light output in all directions. Think of lumens as miles traveled, watts as fuel consumption.

Illuminance (lux) — light landing on a surface. One lux = one lumen per square meter. This is what regulations specify. Luminance (candelas per m²) — light perceived by the eye. This is subjective — each observer perceives it differently.

SCENE 4Two Currents of Design6:00–9:00
[VISUAL: Split screen — regulatory vs. perceptual]

There are two currents in lighting design. The regulatory current — meeting lux standards, calculations, compliance. The perceptual current — how the space actually feels. You can meet every regulation perfectly and still create a terrible space. Regulations deal with illuminance (what hits the surface). Perception deals with luminance (what the eye experiences).

We use both tools: technical calculations for compliance, plus on-site tests for perception. Measurements are made for regulations; perception is made for humans.

SCENE 5The Importance of Testing9:00–10:30
[VISUAL: On-site testing, comparing renders to reality]

You cannot design light entirely from behind a screen. Renders can be adjusted — levels tweaked, luminaires added. But that's not genuine light. 🎬 The Subject, Observer

Duration: ~8 min · 6 scenes

▶

Video 2.2 The Subject, Observer

~8 min

SCENE 1Opening0:00–0:30
[VISUAL: Eye diagram]

We've talked about what light is. But there's a critical piece: the person experiencing it. The observer is just as important as the light source.

SCENE 2The Human Eye0:30–2:30
[VISUAL: Anatomical eye diagram, rods and cones]

Rods — sensitive to brightness, active in low-light (scotopic vision, night vision). Cones — sensitive to color, active in well-lit conditions (photopic vision, full color). Between them: mesopic vision — the transitional state at dusk, in dimmed restaurants. Most interior environments operate here, where perception is most complex and interesting.

SCENE 3Visual Adaptation2:30–4:30
[VISUAL: Dark to bright transitions animated]

Light adaptation (dark to bright) — fast, ~5 seconds. Dark adaptation (bright to dark) — slow, up to 30 minutes. Chromatic adaptation — adjusting to different color temperatures. The transition from a bright lobby to a dim restaurant isn't accidental — it's designed. The moment of dark adaptation becomes part of the experience.

SCENE 4The Active Interpreter4:30–6:30
[VISUAL: Same room, different people, different activities]

The observer is not passive. The same room lit identically can feel different to a 25-year-old and a 70-year-old — the lens yellows with age, requiring more light. A designer designing only for their own eyes designs for one person.

Consider a bedroom: desk work needs high-quality directional light. Reading needs warm, focused light. Meditation may require near-darkness. Each activity demands different lighting.

SCENE 5Perception vs. Measurement6:30–7:30
[VISUAL: Two rooms, identical lux, different feelings]

Two rooms with identical 500 lux readings can feel completely different. Warm indirect light on wood feels cozy. Cool direct light on white feels clinical. Illuminance = quantity. Perception = quality. We design for quality.

🎬 Light, Materials, Objects

Duration: ~11 min · 6 scenes

Video 2.3 Light, Materials, Objects

~11 min

SCENE 1Opening0:00–0:30
[VISUAL: Material samples]

Now comes the crucial third element: the surfaces and materials that light interacts with. Understanding material-light interactions is the foundation of every design decision.

SCENE 2How Light Behaves on Surfaces0:30–2:30
[VISUAL: Animated reflection, absorption, transmission, refraction]

Reflection — specular (mirror), diffuse (matte), spread (satin). Absorption — dark materials absorb more. Transmission — clear glass vs. frosted glass. Refraction — light bends between materials. Every surface does some combination simultaneously. Your job is to predict and orchestrate these interactions.

SCENE 3Material Properties Matter2:30–5:00
[VISUAL: Same light on different materials]

A matte black desk photographed from two angles shows completely different results — reflecting from above, absorbing from the side. White blackout curtains block exterior light but bounce interior light. Dark vinyl paint absorbs excess on sunny days but feels somber on cloudy days. You must visit your space at different times of day.

SCENE 4Texture and Directionality5:00–7:30
[VISUAL: Wall-washing vs. flat light on textured surfaces]

Directional light reveals texture. Diffuse light hides it. Chukum plaster lit from the front looks plain. Graze it — aim light parallel to the wall — and every grain casts a tiny shadow. The surface comes alive. At Madre Café, our chukum walls were the hero, lit with warm indirect wall-washing.

SCENE 5Your Material Audit7:30–9:30
[VISUAL: Material audit worksheet]

For your project space, document: What is the material? What is the finish? What color? How does it react to light? Photograph under morning, afternoon, and evening light. This catalog becomes essential for fixture selection.

SCENE 1Opening0:00–0:30

Welcome to Unit 3: Conceptualization. This is where the real design begins. You'll learn Richard Kelly's foundational concepts and how to translate client needs into the language of light.

SCENE 2Richard Kelly's Three Categories0:30–4:00
[VISUAL: Photos illustrating each category]

1. Ambient Luminescence — general, uniform background. Safe, navigable, open. Cove lighting, indirect LED strips.

2. Focal Glow — directed, concentrated. Creates hierarchy. Track lights, pendants, desk lamps.

3. Play of Brilliants — sparkle, glitter, decorative. Luxury and energy. Chandeliers, candles, neon.

Every well-lit space uses all three. A cozy restaurant: high ambient, medium focal, high brilliants. A modern office: high ambient, high focal, low brilliants.

SCENE 3Translating Client Needs4:00–7:00
[VISUAL: Client conversation scenario]

When a client says "cozy," you must understand their definition. Translate sensations into lighting language. For Madre Café: directed beams, warm temperatures between 2,700 and 3,000 Kelvin, playing between day and night light.

SCENE 4Color Temperature as Concept Tool7:00–9:30
[VISUAL: Kelvin scale with spaces]

2,000K: candlelight. 2,700K: residential. 3,000K: hospitality. 4,000K: offices. Consider materials: 5,000K on gray concrete looks clean. 3,000K on gray concrete looks brownish and dirty. Material and temperature must work together.

SCENE 5Automation9:30–10:30
[VISUAL: Smart control systems]

Lighting concepts aren't static. At Madre Café, lights auto-on at 8 AM at low level, increase as sun intensifies, dim to 30% at sunset. Your concept should account for how lighting will be operated.

SCENE 2What Is Composition?0:30–2:30

SCENE 1Opening0:00–0:30

This is one of the most important lessons. Drawing light is how you communicate your vision. It's not about artistic perfection — it's about clarity of intention.

SCENE 2Color Palette Setup0:30–3:00
[VISUAL: Marker colors with CCT labels]

Blue = very cold (5000K+). White = neutral (4000K) and unlighted surfaces. Yellow = warm (3000K). Amber = very warm (candlelight, 2000K). For neon: use the actual color. Be consistent throughout ALL drawings.

SCENE 3Drawing the Fixture3:00–5:30
[VISUAL: Step-by-step drawing demo]

SCENE 4Installation Details5:30–7:30

SCENE 5Drawing Beams7:30–10:00
[VISUAL: Various beam types drawn]

Narrow beam: tight triangle, small projected circle. Wide beam: broad cone, soft fading edges. Linear source (LED strip): gradient from cove. Omnidirectional: soft radiating glow. Same fixture model in different beam angles — show the client the options.

SCENE 6Adding Direction10:00–11:30
[VISUAL: Directional arrows]

For directional fixtures, show where they should aim. Without direction drawings, the electrician might aim at the wrong wall. Add arrows from fixture toward target.

SCENE 7Madre Café Walkthrough11:30–15:00

[VISUAL: Avatar with examples]
