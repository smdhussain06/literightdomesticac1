SCENE 1Opening0:00–0:30
[VISUAL: Eye diagram]

We've talked about what light is. But there's a critical piece: the person experiencing it. The observer is just as important as the light source.

SCENE 2The Human Eye0:30–2:30
[VISUAL: Anatomical eye diagram, rods and cones]

Rods — sensitive to brightness, active in low-light (scotopic vision, night vision). Cones — sensitive to color, active in well-lit conditions (photopic vision, full color). Between them: mesopic vision — the transitional state at dusk, in dimmed restaurants. Most interior environments operate here, where perception is most complex and interesting.

SCENE 3Visual Adaptation2:30–4:30
[VISUAL: Dark to bright transitions animated]

Light adaptation (dark to bright) — fast, ~5 seconds. Dark adaptation (bright to dark) — slow, up to 30 minutes. Chromatic adaptation — adjusting to different color temperatures. The transition from a bright lobby to a dim restaurant isn't accidental — it's designed. The moment of dark adaptation becomes part of the experience.

SCENE 4The Active Interpreter4:30–6:30
[VISUAL: Same room, different people, different activities]

The observer is not passive. The same room lit identically can feel different to a 25-year-old and a 70-year-old — the lens yellows with age, requiring more light. A designer designing only for their own eyes designs for one person.

Consider a bedroom: desk work needs high-quality directional light. Reading needs warm, focused light. Meditation may require near-darkness. Each activity demands different lighting.

SCENE 5Perception vs. Measurement6:30–7:30
[VISUAL: Two rooms, identical lux, different feelings]

Two rooms with identical 500 lux readings can feel completely different. Warm indirect light on wood feels cozy. Cool direct light on white feels clinical. Illuminance = quantity. Perception = quality. We design for quality.
